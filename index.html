SF
01 — Origin
San Francisco
Bay Area, CA
It starts here.

I was 26 years old, sort of floating. I didn't think I was good at anything, but some people thought I was. A friend recommended me for a job. Before the interview, that friend said to me, "when they ask you about comp, you have to ask for $100k." That seemed insane.

So I practiced, and I rehearsed. And I asked for $90-100k... and then they offered me the job at $95k. I was surprised. And, hell, I showed up ready to work!

The habit: Don't underestimate yourself.
SF → NJ
02 — First Move
Newark, NJ
Port Logistics Group · 3PL
Learning by building.

No formal logistics training. But my boss showed me how to build pricing models in Excel with fixed & variable costs. I got great at building an acceptable model fast. I thought of these as prototype pricing models.

The prototypes weren't great — but they facilitated conversations with customers. That turned out to be the point.

Lesson: All models are wrong, but ones that facilitate a great conversation are very useful.
SF → NJ → BOS
03 — Scale
Boston
Wayfair · E-Commerce
Prototypes as proposals.

At Wayfair I was surrounded by people who built things the right way. But I also experienced a gap - people trust the things they build too much.

The source of truth inventory reports were unreliable, which created dangerous situations in the warehouses.

Lesson: Prototypes are useful because they are perishable; and people believe they are perishable.
SF → NJ → BOS → MSP
04 — Enterprise
Minneapolis
Target · Enterprise Retail
Buy-in matters more than models.

Target was more of the same, but I showed others how to prototype too. And our speed to action was 10x better than other supply chain teams.

Reactions to a rough model are data. You learn more from "that's wrong" than from months of quiet work on the right answer.

Lesson: give stakeholders something to react to. Reactions are your real requirements.
SF → NJ → BOS → MSP → HTF
05 — Now
Hartford, CT
Lyric · AI-First Supply Chain
The loop is faster now.

At Lyric, AI tools compress the prototype cycle from days to minutes. The habit is the same — build to understand, build to communicate — but the speed changes what's possible.

Vibe coding isn't the skill. Knowing what to build is. That still requires domain knowledge and good questions.

Lesson: speed changes the economics of curiosity. Build more, earlier, cheaper.

Why prototype first?
Clients often don't know what they actually want to optimize until they're mid-deployment. A prototype surfaces the real question before you've committed to the platform.
The earning curve
The prototype isn't the opposite of the platform. It's how you earn the conversation that makes the platform worth buying — and worth deploying correctly.
