01 — A bit about me

There are different
ways to live

Some people have a clear mission early — they pick a lane and go deep. That's a real path. It wasn't mine. I spent 18 years moving across domains: logistics, e-commerce, supply chain, writing, teaching. The thread that connected all of it was a habit of building small things to understand big things.

ONE WAY
Know your mission.
Pick a lane early. Go deep. Build authority in one domain. This works — and some of the best careers I've seen are built this way.
ANOTHER WAY
Explore to find it.
Every new domain is a puzzle. You build small things to understand big things. You're always the learner — and that curiosity compounds over time.
WHAT DIDN'T WORK FOR ME
Wait for permission.
Waiting for the perfect dataset. Waiting for sign-off. Waiting until I fully understood before touching anything. By then, someone else had already learned it the fast way.

"The prototype is not a draft of the thing. It is the process by which you figure out what the thing should be."

02 — Lyric

Know what you want
before you build it

Lyric is an AI-first supply chain optimization platform — clients like Coca-Cola and Google use it to make better decisions across their networks. The hardest part of deploying it isn't the software. It's that clients often don't know what they actually want to optimize until they're in the middle of it.

Buy a platform when
  • The problem is well-understood
  • You need scale and reliability
  • You want to focus on decisions, not the engine
Prototype first when
  • You don't fully understand the problem yet
  • Stakeholders disagree on what success looks like
  • You need to make the invisible visible — fast
The prototype isn't the opposite of the platform. It's how you earn the conversation that makes the platform worth buying.
03 — Closing

The prototype earns
the conversation

You're graduating into a world where AI can generate a working simulation in minutes. That changes what's valuable. The skill isn't coding. It's knowing what to build and why — and that still requires domain knowledge and good questions.

// The habit
Before you deploy anything: prototype it. Before you prototype: understand the tradeoff. Before that: ask what the question actually is. Most analytics projects fail at question definition, not execution.
// Buy vs. Build
Platforms like Lyric are powerful when you know what you want. Prototypes are how you figure that out. They're not in tension — the prototype earns the right to use the platform well.
// AI changes the loop
I built this entire lecture with Claude over a few days — including the interactive widgets. The loop is 10x faster. Learn to use it as a thinking partner, not just a code generator.
// The longer game
Every prototype you build proves you understand something. Build enough of them and you have a portfolio of understanding — more durable than any job title.
